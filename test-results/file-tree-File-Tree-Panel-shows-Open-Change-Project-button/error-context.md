Spatial Element. A spatial element is a node in a tree hierarchy that carries two things: a logical identity shared across all spaces, and a spatial definition instantiated in local geometry. Figure 5 shows the example hierarchy with three levels. A room node provides the coarse navigable container, defined by the local room mesh. Object and region nodes represent meaningful entities within that room—furniture, displays, or semantically grouped areas such as a dining zone—registered to authored, tracked, or synchro- nized scene geometry. Affordance nodes capture finer interaction- bearing surfaces within objects, such as Writable on a board or Sittable on a chair. The same Board_1 may hang on the north wall in one room and the south wall in another; the logical identity is shared, but the spatial definition differs. Its output is what travels across spaces in place of raw coordinates. Spatial Relation. Figure 6 shows an example of spatial relations. The relation captures a transferable interaction property between an actor and a referent in the shared hierarchy. Each relation instance binds four components: (1) A subject anchor —a body-relative handle on the actor (e.g., root, chest, hips, hand, and controller) that localizes the relation to the relevant part of the body. (2) A referent —a node resolved from the shared hierarchy (e.g., Board_1 , Sittable ). (3) A support —a geometric construct instantiated from the refer- ent’s spatial definition that serves as the local frame of reference for encoding the relation. (4) A relation value 𝑅 ∈ [ 0 , 1 ] —the actor’s state encoded relative to the support. When 𝑅 = 1 , the actor’s anchor achieves exactly the relative transform defined by the support—looking directly at the referent, or seated on the Sittable surface; smaller values indicate proportionally weaker engagement. Support is what makes a relation transferable across spaces. Whereas the referent identifies what the actor is relating to, the support speci- fies where on that referent the relation is grounded. This distinction is crucial because the same logical referent may be instantiated with different geometries in each space. For example, in Spacing , the relation is not anchored to the object as an undifferentiated whole, but to the specific boundary location or local boundary segment that the actor is approaching (e.g., the left side of a chalkboard). Participants consistently pointed to continuous visibility— rather than avatar realism or interaction fidelity—as the factor most responsible for feeling co-present. For cross-space systems, this suggests that the first-order design target should be keeping the remote partner visible and their behavior readable, ahead of visual realism, which participants accepted at a range of fidelity levels as long as continuity was maintained. This implication also helps explain why continuity-related questionnaire items improved more clearly than broader layout awareness . A system may preserve the feeling that another person is present in the room even without making the full remote layout transparent, so long as the visible behavior remains coherent enough to be interpretable over time. When continuity was sustained, participants began treating the remote avatar as a socially situated body—attempting handshakes, trying to walk side by side, and throwing a chess piece at the partner. This is consistent with prior AR proxemics findings that users regulate interpersonal distance toward virtual agents [17], and suggests that continuous cross-space presence can reach the threshold where proxemic expectations activate. 7.1.2 Shared interaction structure is the minimum unit of common ground. Participants clearly understood the remote partner when both sides could orient around a shared object —suggesting that shared interaction structure matters more than globally matched room geometry. This shared structure is the minimum unit of com- mon ground: it makes remote action interpretable and defines where cross-space interaction can remain usable. In other words, when the common structure spans only a small or awkward region, the rest of the room becomes harder to use as a shared interaction space. 7.1.3 Ambient awareness as a new communication modality. The findings suggest a specific deployment niche for cross-space pres- ence: lightweight ambient awareness among close others. Partici- pants valued this less as a replacement for phone or video calls than as a way to maintain peripheral awareness without demanding full attention. This framing also clarifies the current boundary of the system: it appears more compelling for low-attention connections than for explicit, turn-taking communication. 7.1.4 Selective disclosure as a design requirement for spatial shar- ing. Participants who valued ambient co-presence simultaneously demanded control over what behavior would be shared and with whom—suggesting that selective disclosure is not secondary pol- ish but a structural requirement. Two dimensions emerged: social , where participants accepted sharing with family and close friends but rejected it with colleagues or strangers (P01, P22, P24, P26), and spatial , where one participant proposed dividing the home into public and private zones with explicit access gates (P03). These suggest that cross-space systems need per-zone, per-relationship disclosure controls rather than a single on/off toggle. A separate tension arises from the asymmetry of the information: because receivers see adapted behavior without knowing the source layout, some found movement hard to interpret and described the experience as one-sided (P10). This requires selective ways to ex- pose necessary context without compromising the privacy benefits. 7.2 Limitations and Future Work 7.2.1 Lightweight cues for ambient legibility. Spatialized voice alone did not always suffice; participants expected subtle cues such as am- bient sounds or peripheral indicators. P16 described the absence of footstep sounds—rather than the avatar’s visual appearance—as the primary source of co-presence breakdown: without auditory cues, the avatar behind them was imperceptible until it suddenly entered their field of view, producing a startling “appeared out of nowhere” sensation. This points toward future systems that supplement vi- sual and voice presence with ambient cues—such as footsteps or object-handling sounds—that signal someone’s nearby presence. 7.2.2 Proactive support for shared-element placement. The current system accepts whatever shared-element configuration the user provides, without evaluating whether the resulting shared structure can sustain legible behavior mapping. When shared elements are clustered in a small area, the system must compress the remote user’s movement range into that region. This reactive approach places the full burden of spatial reasoning on the user at setup time, when the consequences of a poor layout are not yet visible. 7.2.3 Handling activity beyond shared structure. A separate run- time challenge arises when a user steps away from shared structure mid-session—walking to an unregistered shelf or engaging with someone not in the system. The system must then decide how to represent or suppress the remote user’s behavior. One possible di- rection is context-sensitive filtering: the system could temporarily render the avatar as unavailable and suppress audio when behavior is no longer grounded in shared structure, similar to camera-off modes in Zoom [9] or Microsoft Teams [29]. 7.2.4 Proxemic awareness in cross-space mapping. The current sys- tem reconstructs avatar position based on spatial relations to shared elements, but does not explicitly preserve interpersonal distance. Participants spontaneously treated the remote avatar as a socially situated body—attempting handshakes, trying to walk side by side, and adjusting their approach distance. These behaviors suggest that future cross-space systems should preserve proxemic relations, not just avoid collisions: maintaining the social meaning of interper- sonal distance even when the underlying rooms differ in scale and layout. 10"
