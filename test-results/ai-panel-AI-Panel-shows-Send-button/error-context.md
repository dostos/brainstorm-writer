ABSTRACT Cross-space presence enables communication in which people re- main in their own rooms while appearing socially present in each other’s spaces. The central challenge is spatial heterogeneity : every- day rooms differ in layout, geometry, and affordances, so directly replaying motion or relying on rigid shared layouts can make be- havior invalid, unreachable, or hard to interpret. We present Spa- tialBlend , a runtime system for cross-space presence based on relational reconstruction : the system continuously extracts a mini- mal set of interaction-relevant spatial relations between the user and nearby objects, then reconstructs avatar behavior that preserves those relations under the target space’s own topology. We imple- ment SpatialBlend in Unity on Meta Quest 3 and evaluate it in a freeform collaboration prototype across an office and a living room with different layouts. A deployment study with 26 participants (13 dyads) shows that continuous shared presence throughout the room improves social presence and perceived adaptation quality. This paper presents SpatialBlend , a runtime system for cross- space presence based on relational reconstruction . Instead of trans- mitting raw poses or mapping coordinates between rooms, Spa- tialBlend asks: what spatial relationships matter in the ongo- ing interaction, and how can they be preserved in a differently shaped space? SpatialBlend addresses this in two steps. First, it watches what the source user is doing—whom they are facing, what they are approaching, how they are positioned relative to nearby objects—and distills this into a compact, portable description of ongoing interaction. Second, it finds the best way to reproduce that interaction in the target space, given its layout and obstacles. The fundamental difficulty is that the avatar must be somewhere at all times, while the source user’s intent unfolds over time—so the system must commit to a physical position before it knows what that position should express. Zone-Based Systems. Zone-oriented systems partition the envi- ronment into authored anchors or public/blended/private regions where simple local mappings suffice [8, 45, 46]. This strategy works for focused interaction around designated objects, but it fragments continuity because the avatar behavior is only defined inside se- lected pockets of the room. Partially Shared Surfaces and Regions. Partially Blended Re- alities and Blended Whiteboard align matched physical surfaces such as tables and whiteboards and let users transition between a small number of blended configurations [15, 16]. Mutual-space optimization methods similarly search for the largest common ac- cessible region across heterogeneous rooms [21]. These systems preserve collaboration within selected shared regions, but they still center the experience on explicitly chosen subspaces rather than continuous room-scale behavior. Constructed Mutual Spaces. MRUnion and SpaceBlender gener- ate union or blended scenes that combine elements from multiple rooms into a synthetic collaboration space [31, 32]. Voronoi Rooms aligns overlapping rooms and dynamically modulates visibility of non-shared regions based on user proximity [39]. Congdon et al. in- stead compute forward mappings between dissimilar floor plans so each user sees the remote avatar embedded in their own room [10]. These approaches restructure or synthesize the shared environment itself, but they require constructing or modifying the shared space rather than adapting behavior within each user’s existing room. 2.2 Avatar Embodiment, Placement, and Locomotion Adaptation Another line of work adapts the avatar rather than the room. Avatar Placement and Embodiment. Mini-Me and teleported co-user placement techniques reposition or rescale remote users to preserve visibility and task fit [23, 33, 35]. Other systems retarget behavior relative to local objects, from deictic gesture retargeting to learned upper-body motion translation in dissimilar environ- ments [19, 22]. These methods are valuable once the target behavior or placement has already been chosen. Locomotion Adaptation. Predict-and-Drive predicts semanti- cally appropriate arrival locations and adjusts locomotion tim- ing across dissimilar spaces [44]. A closely related body of work adapts locomotion itself through redirected walking (RDW). Rather than preserving interaction semantics across heterogeneous spaces, RDW techniques steer users within limited physical environments by manipulating translation, rotation, or curvature gains during movement. Prior work has characterized tolerable and adaptive gain ranges [7, 37], developed reusable RDW libraries and bench- marks [25], and extended RDW to multi-user settings across sep- arate physical spaces [47]. Experience-oriented variants further make redirected locomotion practical in very small spaces by com- bining perceivable redirection with interface support [36]. Other approaches instead impose continuous, often injective, mappings between source and target navigation spaces so that users remain continuously representable beyond authored local regions [10, 18]. An instance observes its local user and transmits a compact rela- tional description to every other space; simultaneously, it receives such descriptions from remote users and reconstructs their avatars under local geometric constraints. We call this process relational reconstruction : SpatialBlend extracts spatial relations from ongo- ing behavior—what the actor is oriented toward, how close it is to nearby referents, how its body is arranged relative to an affordance— and reconstructs a feasible target-side state that preserves those relations under local geometry. The reconstruction procedure comprises three steps. 1 each instance constructs a shared interaction hierarchy (§4.2): a hierar- chy of rooms, objects, and affordances whose nodes share logical identity across spaces while remaining instantiated in each space. Then, at each tick, two online steps run in parallel for every remote user. 2 Relation extraction (§4.3) encodes the source actor’s embod- ied behavior into support-relative relation values over the shared hierarchy. 3 Topology-aware realization (§4.4) takes the incoming relations and solves for a locally feasible target state, producing con- trol signals that existing motion backends—locomotion, blending with upper-body joints, facing, retargeting—consume directly. 4.2 Shared Interaction Hierarchy Before any live behavior can be interpreted, SpatialBlend con- structs a shared interaction hierarchy across participating spaces: a hierarchy of spatial elements that the actor moves through and interacts with. Spatial Element. A spatial element is a node in a tree hierarchy that carries two things: a logical identity shared across all spaces, and a spatial definition instantiated in local geometry. Figure 5 shows the example hierarchy with three levels. A room node provides the coarse navigable container, defined by the local room mesh. Object and region nodes represent meaningful entities within that room—furniture, displays, or semantically grouped areas such as a dining zone—registered to authored, tracked, or synchro- nized scene geometry. Affordance nodes capture finer interaction- bearing surfaces within objects, such as Writable on a board or Sittable on a chair. The same Board_1 may hang on the north wall in one room and the south wall in another; the logical identity is shared, but the spatial definition differs. Spatial Actor. A spatial actor is an embodied entity—a local user or a remote avatar—that moves through the hierarchy. These anchors serve as the attachment points for spatial relations: facing binds to the chest, spacing to the root, affordance pose to the hips or hands, depending on which part of the body is interaction-relevant. As the actor’s behavior becomes more specific—moving from open circulation toward a board, then engaging its writable surface— the relevant subtree of the hierarchy narrows accordingly (high- lighted in Figure 5). Relation extraction then only queries referents within that subtree, keeping the per-tick cost bounded regardless of overall scene size. 4.3 Relation Extraction The relation extractor converts live embodiment into a compact, space-agnostic description of ongoing interaction. Its output is what travels across spaces in place of raw coordinates. Spatial Relation. Figure 6 shows an example of spatial relations. The relation captures a transferable interaction property between an actor and a referent in the shared hierarchy. Each relation instance binds four components: (1) A subject anchor —a body-relative handle on the actor (e.g., root, chest, hips, hand, and controller) that localizes the relation to the relevant part of the body. (2) A referent —a node resolved from the shared hierarchy (e.g., Board_1 , Sittable ). (3) A support —a geometric construct instantiated from the refer- ent’s spatial definition that serves as the local frame of reference for encoding the relation. (4) A relation value 𝑅 ∈ [ 0 , 1 ] —the actor’s state encoded relative to the support. When 𝑅 = 1 , the actor’s anchor achieves exactly the relative transform defined by the support—looking directly at the referent, or seated on the Sittable surface; smaller values indicate proportionally weaker engagement. Support is what makes a relation transferable across spaces. Whereas the referent identifies what the actor is relating to, the support speci- fies where on that referent the relation is grounded. This distinction is crucial because the same logical referent may be instantiated with different geometries in each space. For example, in Spacing , the relation is not anchored to the object as an undifferentiated whole, but to the specific boundary location or local boundary segment that the actor is approaching (e.g., the left side of a chalkboard). When comparing the two conditions, participants consistently iden- tified continuous visibility as the factor that most shaped their sense of sharing a space. One participant said that when the remote partner remained visible throughout the session, it felt like “being together” (P04), while others described this as feeling more like the same room or the same house (P23, P26). In contrast, when the avatar appeared only near shared objects and was absent elsewhere, participants found it harder to sustain this sense: one described the avatar as “appearing and disappearing on repeat,” leaving almost no feeling of shared space (P25), and another tried to shake hands before the failed contact reminded them the two sites were still separate (P21). Similar observations have been reported in other zone-based systems, where avatars that fade out between zones can reduce spatial awareness [12]. Together, these contrasts sug- gest that uninterrupted avatar presence was the primary driver of the co-presence, trackability, and spatial coherence gains ob- served in the questionnaire. Notably, participants accepted this continuity across a range of avatar styles—some preferred cartoon avatars while others wanted more realistic ones—suggesting that continuous visibility mattered more than the specific level of avatar realism. 6.3.2 Shared elements anchored legibility, but interaction extended between them. Participants most easily interpreted the remote part- ner’s behavior near shared elements, such as the whiteboard and transferred objects (P02, P14, P19). However, many participants described interactions that extended well beyond these anchors. One participant had expected interaction to be confined to the area around shared objects and was surprised to find that the avatar’s movement was well reflected across the whole room (P02). Others described reading the remote partner’s intent from body motion alone—not direct communication, but enough to understand what they were trying to do (P07, P25). Several noted that simply walk- ing around the room together, or being aware that the partner was somewhere in the space doing something, felt valuable even without a shared task (P04, P18, P26). At the same time, certain boundary moments made the separate- space reality immediately apparent. One participant tried to shake hands with the remote partner; the failed contact reminded them that the two sites were still physically separate (P21). The researcher also observed a participant attempting to take the remote partner by the hand and walk together. These moments suggest that co- presence was maintained as long as interactions remained within the shared structure that the system could bridge. 6.3.3 Participants anticipated low-attention use with close others. When asked about future use, participants most often mentioned family, close friends, or long-distance partners. Participants consistently pointed to continuous visibility— rather than avatar realism or interaction fidelity—as the factor most responsible for feeling co-present. For cross-space systems, this suggests that the first-order design target should be keeping the remote partner visible and their behavior readable, ahead of visual realism, which participants accepted at a range of fidelity levels as long as continuity was maintained. This implication also helps explain why continuity-related questionnaire items improved more clearly than broader layout awareness . A system may preserve the feeling that another person is present in the room even without making the full remote layout transparent, so long as the visible behavior remains coherent enough to be interpretable over time. When continuity was sustained, participants began treating the remote avatar as a socially situated body—attempting handshakes, trying to walk side by side, and throwing a chess piece at the partner. This is consistent with prior AR proxemics findings that users regulate interpersonal distance toward virtual agents [17], and suggests that continuous cross-space presence can reach the threshold where proxemic expectations activate. 7.1.2 Shared interaction structure is the minimum unit of common ground. Participants clearly understood the remote partner when both sides could orient around a shared object —suggesting that shared interaction structure matters more than globally matched room geometry. This shared structure is the minimum unit of com- mon ground: it makes remote action interpretable and defines where cross-space interaction can remain usable. In other words, when the common structure spans only a small or awkward region, the rest of the room becomes harder to use as a shared interaction space. 7.1.3 Ambient awareness as a new communication modality. The findings suggest a specific deployment niche for cross-space pres- ence: lightweight ambient awareness among close others. Partici- pants valued this less as a replacement for phone or video calls than as a way to maintain peripheral awareness without demanding full attention. This framing also clarifies the current boundary of the system: it appears more compelling for low-attention connections than for explicit, turn-taking communication. 7.1.4 Selective disclosure as a design requirement for spatial shar- ing. Participants who valued ambient co-presence simultaneously demanded control over what behavior would be shared and with whom—suggesting that selective disclosure is not secondary pol- ish but a structural requirement. Two dimensions emerged: social , where participants accepted sharing with family and close friends but rejected it with colleagues or strangers (P01, P22, P24, P26), and spatial , where one participant proposed dividing the home into public and private zones with explicit access gates (P03). These suggest that cross-space systems need per-zone, per-relationship disclosure controls rather than a single on/off toggle. A separate tension arises from the asymmetry of the information: because receivers see adapted behavior without knowing the source layout, some found movement hard to interpret and described the experience as one-sided (P10). This requires selective ways to ex- pose necessary context without compromising the privacy benefits. 7.2 Limitations and Future Work 7.2.1 Lightweight cues for ambient legibility. Spatialized voice alone did not always suffice; participants expected subtle cues such as am- bient sounds or peripheral indicators. P16 described the absence of footstep sounds—rather than the avatar’s visual appearance—as the primary source of co-presence breakdown: without auditory cues, the avatar behind them was imperceptible until it suddenly entered their field of view, producing a startling “appeared out of nowhere” sensation. This points toward future systems that supplement vi- sual and voice presence with ambient cues—such as footsteps or object-handling sounds—that signal someone’s nearby presence. 7.2.2 Proactive support for shared-element placement. The current system accepts whatever shared-element configuration the user provides, without evaluating whether the resulting shared structure can sustain legible behavior mapping. When shared elements are clustered in a small area, the system must compress the remote user’s movement range into that region. This reactive approach places the full burden of spatial reasoning on the user at setup time, when the consequences of a poor layout are not yet visible. 7.2.3 Handling activity beyond shared structure. A separate run- time challenge arises when a user steps away from shared structure mid-session—walking to an unregistered shelf or engaging with someone not in the system. The system must then decide how to represent or suppress the remote user’s behavior. One possible di- rection is context-sensitive filtering: the system could temporarily render the avatar as unavailable and suppress audio when behavior is no longer grounded in shared structure, similar to camera-off modes in Zoom [9] or Microsoft Teams [29]. 7.2.4 Proxemic awareness in cross-space mapping. The current sys- tem reconstructs avatar position based on spatial relations to shared elements, but does not explicitly preserve interpersonal distance. Participants spontaneously treated the remote avatar as a socially situated body—attempting handshakes, trying to walk side by side, and adjusting their approach distance. These behaviors suggest that future cross-space systems should preserve proxemic relations, not just avoid collisions: maintaining the social meaning of interper- sonal distance even when the underlying rooms differ in scale and layout. 10"

If the target-side geodesic separation satisfies 𝑑 𝑔 ( 𝑎 ′ , 𝑏 ′ ) > 2 𝑣 max ( 𝑇 − 𝑡 𝑑 ) , (6) where 𝑇 is the source arrival time, then no causal realization policy can guarantee all three of the following simultaneously for both tra- jectories: (i) conservative commitment, (ii) physical plausibility, and (iii) temporal synchrony. Proof sketch. Because 𝜉 𝑎 and 𝜉 𝑏 are identical up to 𝑡 𝑑 , any causal policy receives the same source evidence on [ 0 , 𝑡 𝑑 ] in both cases and must therefore occupy the same target-side state 𝑆 𝑑 at time 𝑡 𝑑 . To remain temporally synchronized while respecting the motion bound 𝑣 max , that state must be able to reach both 𝑎 ′ and 𝑏 ′ by time 𝑇 . Equivalently, 𝑆 𝑑 must lie in the intersection of the two target-side reachable sets of radius 𝑣 max ( 𝑇 − 𝑡 𝑑 ) centered at 𝑎 ′ and 𝑏 ′ . Condition (6) makes these reachable sets disjoint. Hence no such common state exists. The policy is therefore forced into one of three failure modes. It may commit toward one referent before 𝑡 𝑑 , sacrificing conservative commitment if the early guess is wrong. It may wait until disam- biguation and then move naturally, sacrificing temporal synchrony because arrival becomes late. Or it may wait and still attempt to arrive on time, sacrificing physical plausibility by requiring non- physical correction.
